When relationships get stuck

Often love has not disappeared. But the old way of relating no longer works.

Counseling may be helpful when you do not just want to “communicate better” again, but want to understand the cycle that keeps bringing you back to the same situation.

You keep falling into the same pattern.

One person pushes, one withdraws. One explains, one shuts down. Both actually want contact — and yet end up back in the old cycle.

You want clarity without rushing into a decision.

Stay, leave, open up, close, begin again: Sometimes it takes a space where ambivalence is allowed before a direction becomes visible.

You want to speak more honestly without causing harm.

Difficult truths need a form. We work on saying more clearly what is going on — while staying respectful and connected.

Your relationship does not fit into conventional categories.

Monogamous, open, polyamorous, queer or still unnamed: Come as you are. This is not primarily about right or wrong, but about what feels coherent, safe and responsible for you or for all of you.

Relationship is adventure. Work. And a mirror.

Sometimes people love each other — and still hurt each other.

Relationship is often taken for granted, as if we simply knew how to do it: through intuition, experience or love. But closeness, conflict, repair, boundaries and trust are skills that are allowed to grow.

Many people do not come to counseling because everything is already lost. They come because they notice: our old strategies no longer help. Being right, silence, explaining, withdrawal, pressure, adaptation — at some point these become a pattern.

Counseling can help slow this pattern down and make it visible: What is happening right now? What is being protected? What is being avoided? And what could be a next step that is more honest and at the same time more connecting?

My approach

Clear enough to be honest. Safe enough to stay open.

Counseling is not about making one person the guilty one. It is about understanding the shared pattern: What happens between you? What does it trigger in you? And what do you then do that may further intensify the situation?

I work directly, respectfully and with a focus on relationship. Difficult things may be spoken — not in order to hurt, but to come back into contact with yourself and with each other.

Self-respect, boundaries and connection matter to me: standing up for yourself without devaluing others; taking responsibility without sinking into shame; seeking closeness without creating pressure.

Polyamory, open relationships & relationship diversity

Come as you are. There is no simple right or wrong.

Some people come to counseling because they live in an open or polyamorous relationship. Others because they are thinking about it. Others again because a relationship is reaching its limits around questions of freedom, commitment, jealousy, sexuality or honesty.

Consent & agreements

Open and polyamorous relationships do not need less commitment, but often more conscious agreements: What applies? What is changing? What needs safety, transparency and mutual consent?

Jealousy & emotional safety

Jealousy, fear, shame or insecurity are not automatically signs that something is “wrong”. They can point to places where needs, boundaries, attachment and self-respect want to be looked at more closely.

Multi-person settings

Depending on the concern, counseling can make sense as an individual, couple or multi-person setting. In the introductory call we clarify together which frame fits your situation.

My background

Relationship can be diverse — and still needs clarity, safety and good agreements.

In my master’s thesis, I worked on counseling in the context of polyamory: emotional dynamics, societal expectations, uncertainties in counseling situations and the question of what professional support needs so that people do not have to explain or justify themselves.

My aim is not to tell you the “right” way to do relationship. We look together at what is coherent, responsible and livable for you and your relationship(s) — and what might enable more clarity, safety or connection right now.

About me

I am interested in relationship where it is allowed to become honest.

My name is Theophil Kroller. In my work, I aim to create a space where there is room for what otherwise often remains unspoken: uncertainty, longing, anger, shame, withdrawal, hope.

I have been shaped by psychosocial counseling, my master’s thesis on polyamory and my time in China and Japan. These experiences sharpened my awareness of how differently people can understand closeness, responsibility, boundaries and belonging.

You decide how you want to live relationship. Whether classically monogamous, polyamorous, queer or somewhere in between — I accompany you openly, respectfully and without quick judgment.

Frequently asked questions

What you can know beforehand

Does my partner have to come with me?

No. You can also start alone. Sometimes individual counseling is a useful first step to see your own situation, boundaries and possibilities for action more clearly.

Is counseling also suitable for open or polyamorous relationships?

Yes. Open, polyamorous, queer or not clearly named relationship forms are explicitly welcome. We look at your concrete situation — not at a predefined ideal.

Is this psychotherapy?

No. My offer is psychosocial counseling. It does not replace psychotherapy, medical treatment or acute crisis or emergency care. Counseling starts where you want to regain more orientation, clarity and room for action — for example in relationship patterns, communication, boundaries, decisions and next steps.

What happens in the introductory call?

Together we sort out your concern, discuss the appropriate setting and see whether my approach fits your situation.
